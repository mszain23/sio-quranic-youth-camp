Drama, Skit & Reels

Purposeful storytelling for campus youth

Purpose of Drama in Campus

Drama and skits are powerful tools to express values, address social issues, and inspire action. When used well, they can educate, emotionally move, and motivate audiences to think deeply about important topics — faith, justice, and moral conduct.

"Drama is not just performance — it's purposeful storytelling. Put your heart into it, and let your message shine through."

Choosing the Right Topic

  • Choose a topic relevant to youth that aligns with the goals of the camp
  • It should offer space for emotional depth, a clear message, and creative elements
  • The topic should involve conflict-resolution, moral clarity, or social insight
Example Topics: A friend questioning their Deen · Injustice on campus · Dawah to non-Muslims · Addiction to digital content · Peer pressure and identity

Building the Story Arc

Beginning
Introduce the setting and characters. Establish the world and who the people are.
Conflict
Present the challenge or dilemma — the problem that needs to be resolved.
Climax
The most intense moment — confrontation, a decision, or the turning point.
Resolution
The moral or outcome — ideally hopeful or instructive. Leave the audience thinking.

Writing the Script

  • Clearly label scenes, dialogue, and stage directions
  • Keep dialogue natural and age-appropriate
  • Use short, impactful lines — avoid long monologues unless necessary
  • Include humour, suspense, or emotion where relevant
  • End each scene with clarity — what should the audience take away?
SCENE 2 — Campus Bench, Late Afternoon
ALI: (sits with phone, scrolling) I don't know, man. Faith feels like a phase we've outgrown.
HAMZA: Or maybe it's the phase we never really understood. (pauses, looks at him) Want to talk?
ALI: (puts phone down slowly) ...

Rehearsal & Production

Rehearsals
Rehearse each scene separately before full run-throughs. Assign one team member as director.
Stage Planning
Define entry and exit points. Practice scene transitions smoothly. Keep props simple.
Props & Costumes
Use minimal yet meaningful props. Costumes can be basic — just enough to show character identity.
Timing
Target 7–10 minutes. Every minute should add value — avoid dragging scenes.

Acting Techniques & Engagement

  • Voice modulation — adjust pitch and tone to match emotion
  • Facial expressions — practice in front of a mirror
  • Body language — use gestures, posture, and movement to show character
  • Don't overact — stay believable; use pauses, eye contact, and volume strategically
  • Open with a strong hook — dialogue or visual that grabs attention immediately
  • End powerfully — close with an emotionally resonant or thought-provoking line
  • Audience impact — think from their perspective; leave them with a question or a call to action
